What is a DevOps Dojo?

From Wikipedia: 

A dōjō (道場) is a hall or space for immersive learning or meditation. This is traditionally in the field of martial arts, but has been seen increasingly in other fields, such as meditation and software development. The term literally means "place of the Way" in Japanese.

In line with the definition, a DevOps Dojo is a place and a process for immersive learning on DevOps topics.  

DevOps Dojo Principles

Listed below are some of the principals of a DevOps Dojo.  These have been learned by Dojos and their coaches through experience.

  • Training without doing is hard to absorb
  • Transform the operating model to focus on value delivered and not on an arbitrary deliver date
  • Engaged teams must be full stack teams only
  • Engagements are typically six weeks in length and work hours are 8:00 to 5:00
  • Teams are typically 6-10 people per team
  • Sprints are done twice weekly in 2.5 day intervals
  • Full scrum ceremonies are still performed
  • Teams are fully co-located at the Dojo and 100% dedicated to the Dojo process with no other external meetings allowed during the engagement period, other than those the Dojo team initiates.

DevOps Dojo Requirements

What do I need to get a DevOps Dojo started?

  • A coach
    • Coaches ideally should have an agile, cloud and/or DevOps background
  • A place to practice
  • Students
  • Video conference capability and/or a large shared monitor
  • Post-it notes and large paper tables
  • Markers

Value Generation and Measurement

  • Investments in training can be sustained by providing the business with a clear cost justification
  • Collecting and reporting on metrics are the key to successful long term Dojo success
  • CapitalOne's Hygieia OpenSource project is a great tool for collecting and reporting on DevOps metrics
  • XLRelease will also collect and report on DevOps metrics
  • A successful approach is to "gamify" around the metrics provided by Hygieia
    • Good behavior should be rewarded
    • Teams helping other teams transform from bad to good should be rewarded

CTP Team Approach

  • Initial Engagement - Rapid Improvement and Learning
  • Team Onboarding Assessment

Typical Challenges for Dojos in Large Organizations

  • Remote teams
  • Global staff
  • No common baseline skills amongst teams
  • Pervasive technical debt
  • Finding alignment with the purpose of the Dojo
